The Core Idea

The Flipbook Method

A video file doesn't contain motion. It contains pictures, 30 or 60 of them for every second, and your eye invents the movement. A 15 second clip is roughly 900 still pages. That one fact is the entire trick: every edit in this system is just an instruction applied to pages of a flipbook, and because it's an instruction, it lands with perfect precision on exactly the pages you name.

Graphics on exact pagesStamp a title card, brand bar, logo, or end card onto precisely the pages you name. On at page 36, gone at page 78, never a frame early or late
Captions and karaokeBurn every spoken word onto the pages where it's said, word-by-word highlighting included. The reason sound-off viewers still get the whole story
Fake camera movesCut a smaller window out of each page and move the window. Slide it: pan. Shrink it: push-in. Jiggle it: handheld. The camera never existed
Speed tricksSkip every other page: double speed. Print pages twice: slow motion. Flip the stack: reverse
Glitch surgeryRemove the three bad pages an AI gen morphed and the stack closes seamlessly around them
Page extractionPull the last page as a still to feed your next gen's first frame, or lay out 20 pages as a contact sheet

What it's most used for: burning captions and text hooks, dropping brand bars and end cards onto finished gens, frame-accurate trims that scissor out morphing artifacts, camera moves added after the fact, freezes, loops, and stills for continuation chains. Anywhere a normal editor would scrub and eyeball, the flipbook method counts pages instead, and it never misses by a frame.

The one thing this can't do: invent footage. This system edits video that already exists. Your generators (Seedance, Kling, Veo) create the footage; this pipeline finishes it. And because the whole edit is written instructions instead of clicks, the same recipe can run on one clip or a folder of two hundred, overnight, unattended.

How This Actually Works

The AI can't watch or film video. It edits by writing instructions for tools that live on a small computer attached to the chat. Here's the whole thing, no jargon.

Step 1

Look at your video

ffprobe

Every video file carries an ID card inside it: how big, how long, how many frames per second. The AI reads that card, then grabs a few snapshot photos from the clip so it knows what it's working with. It never watches the video. It reads the paperwork and peeks at stills.

▼
Step 2

Agree on the plan

You + the AI

The AI lists the creative choices out loud: what text, what style, where it goes, crop or no crop. Nothing renders until you say go.

▼
Step 3

Make the pieces

Python + fonts

Anything that needs to be drawn gets made first, like digital construction paper: title cards, brand bars, caption files, fonts fetched fresh. These are the stickers that get placed on your video later.

▼
Step 4

Write the recipe

Filtergraph

Instead of dragging clips around in an editor, the AI writes one set of instructions: "do this to every frame." The instructions pull from 555 built-in effects, and every single one is one of four flavors:

StampPut things on top: text, captions, logos, bars
ChopCut, reorder, speed up, fake camera moves
PaintChange the look: color, grain, blur, green screen
MeasureCheck things without changing anything
▼
Step 5

Press go

ffmpeg

ffmpeg is a robot editor with no imagination and perfect obedience. It runs the recipe on every single frame, about 900 of them in a 15-second clip, and builds the new video. If it hits an error, the AI reads the error, fixes the recipe, and reruns. You never see this part.

▼
Step 6

Check the work by math

Python

The AI still can't watch the result, so it proves the edit worked with numbers: pull a frame from second 1, count the white pixels where the text should be, confirm they're there and gone again by second 3. If the numbers are wrong, back to the recipe.

▼
Step 7

You watch it

Your eyes

You get the finished file. The math proved the edit ran. Whether it looks good is a call only a human can make, and it's yours. Not right? Say what's off and the loop runs again, seconds not hours.

Claude Pipeline Cheat Layer

Canonical, copy-paste-safe invocations for the filters most relevant to vertical short-form automation in THIS container (ffmpeg 6.1.1, no GPU). Feed this doc to Claude as context; these patterns override guesswork. All follow the tested gotcha rules documented in the tables below.

Format & delivery

  • Vertical 1080x1920 letterbox: -vf "scale=1080:-2,pad=1080:1920:(ow-iw)/2:(oh-ih)/2:black,fps=30,format=yuv420p"
  • Blurred-background vertical: -filter_complex "[0]split[a][b];[a]scale=1080:1920,boxblur=20[bg];[b]scale=1080:-2[fg];[bg][fg]overlay=(W-w)/2:(H-h)/2,format=yuv420p"
  • End every RGB-touching chain with format=yuv420p before H.264 encode

Captions & text

  • Burn ASS (libass, full styling): -vf "ass=captions.ass"
  • Burn SRT with style: -vf "subtitles=subs.srt:force_style='FontName=Montserrat,Fontsize=18,Outline=2'"
  • drawtext: use textfile=title.txt, never inline text with colons/quotes

Cuts, speed, joins

  • Trim segment: trim=start=3:end=8,setpts=PTS-STARTPTS (audio: atrim=...,asetpts=PTS-STARTPTS) - the reset is MANDATORY
  • 2x speed: -filter_complex "[0:v]setpts=0.5*PTS[v];[0:a]atempo=2.0[a]" (atempo per-instance 0.5-2.0; chain for more)
  • Transition: normalize both clips first (scale,setsar=1,fps=30), then xfade=transition=fade:duration=1:offset=CLIP1_DURATION_MINUS_1 + acrossfade=d=1
  • Join same-codec files: concat DEMUXER (-f concat -safe 0 -i list.txt -c copy); different codecs/sizes: concat FILTER with n=/v=/a= counts

Audio

  • Platform loudness: -af "loudnorm=I=-16:TP=-1.5:LRA=11,aresample=48000" (two-pass with measured_* values for final masters; never TP=0)
  • Music under voice: amix=inputs=2:duration=shortest:weights='1 0.3' or sidechaincompress for real ducking
  • Missing audio track fix: -f lavfi -i anullsrc=r=48000:cl=stereo + -shortest
  • Fades: afade=t=out:st=START:d=2 with video fade=t=out:st=START:d=2

Images & motion

  • Ken Burns: upscale FIRST: scale=8000:-1,zoompan=z='min(zoom+0.0015,1.5)':x='iw/2-(iw/zoom/2)':y='ih/2-(ih/zoom/2)':d=150:s=1080x1920:fps=30
  • GIF (always two-pass): palettegen then paletteuse (or split version: [0]split[a][b];[a]palettegen[p];[b][p]paletteuse)

Polish

  • Denoise+sharpen finish: hqdn3d=3:3:6:6,cas=0.4
  • Grade with LUT: lut3d=file.cube:interp=tetrahedral
  • Greenscreen: [1]chromakey=0x00FF00:0.25:0.1[k];[0][k]overlay,despill=green - despill is not optional

Detection (script decision hooks)

  • Scene cuts: scdet=t=15,metadata=print (select's scene score is a DIFFERENT 0-1 scale)
  • Dead air: silencedetect=n=-35dB:d=0.8 | Black/fades: blackdetect | Frozen: freezedetect
  • Verify loudness: -af ebur128 -f null -

Geometry & Transform 33

These filters change the shape and framing of the picture itself: making it bigger or smaller, cutting off the edges, adding borders, flipping it like a mirror, or rotating it. Think of them as everything you would do to a physical photo with scissors, a copier, and your hands. Reach for these when you need to resize for a platform (like 9:16 vertical for TikTok), reframe a shot, or fix a video that came in sideways.

Blur, Sharpen & Denoise 39

These filters control how crisp or soft the image feels. Blur softens detail (great for backgrounds or hiding things), sharpening makes edges pop so footage looks crisper, and denoising removes the grainy speckle that shows up in low-light or heavily compressed video. A light denoise before upload is one of the oldest tricks for making footage survive a platform's compression better.

How to Prompt

5 Part AnatomyCopy Paste TemplatesDo and Don'tThe Fix Loop

LLMs are genuinely good at writing ffmpeg, good enough that this pattern has replaced search-and-paste for most people. But the difference between one clean render and an afternoon of broken commands is how you ask. These rules come from community testing and from building this exact system.

The 5 parts of a working edit prompt

1. The file and its facts

Upload the clip and say what it is. Then let the model read it with ffprobe instead of trusting your memory: "Run ffprobe first and tell me the specs before doing anything." Resolution, frame rate, and duration drive every decision downstream.

2. The intent

Purpose, platform, audience, and what counts as a win. "12 second history TikTok, the lesson lives in the caption" produces different, better recommendations than no context, and it steers the web research pass toward your genre. Skip this and you get generic advice optimized for a video that doesn't exist.

3. The edit, with timestamps

Exact asks beat vibes, because every ask becomes a filter: hooks are drawtext, captions are the subtitles filter reading a whisper-cli SRT, moves are a crop window, all chained in one filter_complex. "Kinetic text hook from 0 to 2 seconds, captions throughout, do not trim anything" renders on the first pass. "Make it pop" renders nothing you wanted. If you know the moment, give the number.

4. The constraints

Fonts by name (a real TTF gets fetched from GitHub, so name it exactly), colors by hex, on-screen text in exact quotes (it goes into a textfile so punctuation can't break the command), and everything already rejected. State hard requirements as hard: "Montserrat Bold, #E84393 accent, keep the full runtime." A constraint the model doesn't know about is a rerender you'll be waiting for.

5. The proof

Ask for verification, not vibes: "Verify each change with a measurement and show me the numbers." That means ffprobe on the output plus a Python pixel count on extracted frames proving the text is there at 1s and gone at 3s. A command exiting cleanly is not the same as the edit existing in the file. Demand the numbers.

Do and don't

✓ Do
  • Make it check the filter menu before promising anything: "confirm the filter exists in this build first"
  • Ask for one filter chain, one encode: "single pass, all effects in one filter_complex"
