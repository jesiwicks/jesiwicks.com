Prompt as Blueprint Cinematic Language Google Flow

Veo 3 is trained to understand cinematic language - camera angles, lighting, shot composition, dialogue, sound design. The more precisely you describe the scene like a director writing a brief, the more accurately it executes your vision. Your prompt is not a wish. It is a film instruction.

The Concept That Changes Your Results

Vague prompts guess.
Directed prompts execute.

Most people treat Veo 3 like a search engine - type what you want and hope for the best. It responds to something more specific: the same language a director uses on set. Shot size, lens, camera move, lighting motivation, audio cues. The more precisely you describe those elements, the less the model has to guess - and the closer your output is to what you envisioned.

Vague - the model guesses

"A person walking in a rainy city at night. Cinematic."

What person? Which city? What camera angle? What does "cinematic" mean here? The model fills every gap with its own interpretation. You get a result - just not necessarily yours.

Directed - the model executes

Medium shot. A tired office worker, shoulders hunched, walking slowly under a neon sign. Cold rain. Camera holds steady, then slow push-in. Audio: rain on concrete, distant traffic. No subtitles.

Each element removes a decision the model would otherwise make on its own. Shot size tells it how much of the frame the subject occupies. The camera move is explicit rather than implied. Audio cues are named, not left to inference. The model executes the brief rather than writing one.

The official Google formula
Cinematography + Subject + Action + Context + Style & Ambiance Cinematography first. It sets tone and emotion for everything that follows.
Google Flow | Your Veo 3 Filmmaking Workspace

Four generation modes.
Pick the right one for the shot.

Google Flow is Google's dedicated AI filmmaking workspace built around Veo 3. Every generation starts in one of four modes - which one you choose determines how much of the visual direction you control vs. what you leave to the model. Start in Text to Video and move to the other modes as your workflow develops.

Mode 01
Text to Video

Describe the scene. Veo builds it with audio.

The default mode. Type your prompt using the cinematic formula - Cinematography, Subject, Action, Context, Style - and Veo generates the clip complete with synchronized audio. The best place to start every project and test your prompt before committing to reference-based workflows.
Use when → building from scratch, testing a scene concept, establishing shots
Mode 02
Ingredients to Video

Pin characters, objects, or styles across shots.

Upload up to 3 reference images - a character, an object, a location - and describe how they interact in your prompt. Veo anchors its generation to those references, including audio. The primary tool for character continuity across multiple clips.
Use when → recurring characters, branded products, consistent visual style
Mode 03
Frames to Video

Lock the start and end. Veo fills the middle.

Supply a start image, an end image, or both. Veo generates the motion between them with audio. You own both endpoints - the model only controls the transition. Strong for scene reveals, smooth shot transitions, and keeping a character's appearance locked at the frame level.
Use when → product reveals, scene transitions, character accuracy matters most
Mode 04
Extend

Continue any clip. Chain up to 148 seconds.

Take any generated clip and continue it forward. Each Scenebuilder extension adds approximately 7 seconds. Starting from an 8-second clip, chaining 20 extensions gives you up to 148 seconds total.
⚠ Use Scenebuilder Extend only - the Extend button under a generated clip uses Veo 2, no audio
Use when → a shot needs more time, building long sequences, chaining scenes
Keeping Characters Consistent | What Actually Works

Veo 3 doesn't drift randomly.
It drifts predictably.

Character consistency is Veo 3's most cited limitation - and it's real. But it follows patterns. Understanding those patterns means you can work around them deliberately rather than regenerating and hoping. These are the fixes that practitioners have verified across extended production use.

Fix 01
Use 2-3 reference images with consistent lighting
Upload clear character references from different angles but keep lighting temperature the same across all of them. Mixing warm and cool references causes the model to alternate between interpretations. If the character drifts, swap the weakest reference image first.
Fix 02
State camera movement explicitly - or the model improvises
If you don't specify movement, Veo defaults to whatever it infers from training data for that scene type. Write it as a standalone sentence: The camera holds steady. or Slow dolly push-in. This reduces the model's degrees of freedom and keeps identity stable.
Fix 03
One dominant action per clip
Practitioners consistently find that prompting multiple simultaneous actions - walking, speaking, and gesturing in one clip - produces less stable identity results than giving each clip one clear action. Build the sequence by chaining single-action clips in Scenebuilder rather than packing complexity into one generation.
Fix 04
Use Frames to Video to lock both endpoints
When character accuracy matters most, supply both a start and end frame from your reference set. With both endpoints pinned, the model only generates the motion between them - dramatically reducing how much it can reinterpret the character mid-clip.
The verbatim rule - repeat character descriptions exactly When the same character appears across multiple clips, copy and paste their description word-for-word into every prompt. Don't summarize or rephrase - only change the action and camera. Practitioner testing shows that using complete, identical character descriptions versus simplified ones can improve consistency by up to 40%. The model needs the same wording in the same form to hold the same identity.

What You Can Input

Four input types.
Audio is in the prompt.

Veo 3 accepts four input types: text prompts, reference images, frame images, and audio direction. That last one is different from every other AI video model - there is no audio file upload field. Audio is generated natively from words you write in the prompt. Describe the sound, and Veo builds it alongside the video in a single pass.

Required

3 Skill Levels Copy-Paste Prompts Veo 3 for TikTok

Three levels of Veo 3 prompts - from your first text-to-video generation to chaining multi-shot sequences in Google Flow. Level 1 needs nothing but a prompt. Level 2 is where Veo 3 gets unique: making characters talk with native audio. Level 3 is multi-shot production. Every template is copy-paste ready and tested against the model.

The Prompt Formula

Five parts.
In this order.

Every effective Veo 3 prompt follows Google's official structure. The order matters - Cinematography comes first because it sets tone and emotional direction for everything that follows. Learn this once and every template below will make immediate sense.

The Official Google Veo 3 Prompt Structure
Cinematography + Subject + Action + Context + Style & Ambiance
Lead with the camera - shot size, lens, movement. Then describe the subject, what they're doing, where they are, and how it should feel. Audio lives in Style & Ambiance: describe sounds in plain language, dialogue after a colon. One camera move per prompt. Be specific about speed: "slow dolly" not "dramatic."

Level 2 | Dialogue & Audio Direction
Level 2
Make Veo 3 talk | Veo 3's unique capability
10 minutes · Gemini app or Google Flow · Goal: generate a clip where a character speaks with synced audio

No other AI video model generates synchronized dialogue and audio natively. When Veo 3 renders your clip, the character's voice, lip movement, and every ambient sound in the scene are all generated together in the same pass - not added afterwards. The one catch: how you write the dialogue line in the prompt determines whether you get clean synced speech or unwanted burnt-in subtitles. One punctuation rule fixes that entirely.

The dialogue rules - read before generating Write dialogue after a colon, not in quotes: She says: Your line here. Quotes tell Veo 3 to render on-screen text. A colon tells it to generate spoken audio. Always add No subtitles. at the end of any prompt with speech. Keep lines to 20 words or fewer - a clip is 8 seconds, and more than 20 words means the character has to rush to fit it in.

Level 3 | Multi-Shot Production
Level 3
Chaining clips in Google Flow Scenebuilder
30–45 minutes · Requires Google Flow · Goal: a 3-shot sequence with a consistent character and matched audio

Veo 3 caps every generation at 8 seconds. To make something longer, you build a sequence - multiple clips chained together in Google Flow's Scenebuilder tool, where each new shot starts from the last frame of the previous one. The result looks continuous even though it's separate generations. The only thing that breaks it: letting your character description change between shots. Keep it identical and the sequence holds. Change a single word and the character starts to drift.

Phase A — Before you generate
Step 1
Write a character block and don't change it
A character block is a single sentence describing your character's age, hair color and length, skin tone, and what they're wearing. Write it once, copy it word-for-word to the top of every prompt in your sequence. Never paraphrase it - the model treats even minor wording changes as a different person.
Maya: early 30s, short dark hair, olive skin, cream linen shirt. Keep all features unchanged.
Verbatim reuse improves consistency up to 40%
Step 2
Generate Shot 1 on Veo 3 Fast until the look is exactly right
Use Veo 3 Fast (20 credits per generation) for all draft work. Run 2–3 variations, tweaking one thing per run - prompt wording, lighting description, camera direction. Change only one thing at a time so you know what caused the improvement. Shot 1 sets the visual template for the whole sequence - the lighting, color grade, and character look established here carry through to every shot that follows.
Model: Veo 3 Fast 20 credits / gen
Phase B — Building the sequence
Step 3
Pin the last frame of Shot 1 as the start of Shot 2
In Google Flow Scenebuilder, hover over the very last frame of Shot 1 and click the + icon - this saves that frame as a reusable asset. You'll use it as the opening frame for Shot 2 in the next step. Connecting shots this way means Shot 2 picks up visually exactly where Shot 1 left off - same lighting, same environment, same character position.
Step 4
Generate Shot 2 using Frames to Video
In Scenebuilder, select Frames to Video and set the saved frame from Step 3 as your start frame. Write the prompt with your character block first, then the new action for this shot only - don't re-describe anything already established by the start frame.
[Character block]. [New action + camera move]. Match color grade and lighting from start frame. No subtitles.
Repeat Steps 3 and 4 for every additional shot in the sequence.
Step 5
Re-render the full sequence on Veo 3 Quality
Once you're happy with the full sequence at Fast quality, re-render each shot individually on Veo 3 Quality (100 credits per generation) for the final output. Render one shot at a time - if a shot comes out wrong on Quality, you only need to redo that one shot rather than the whole sequence.
Model: Veo 3 Quality 100 credits / gen

5 Common Mistakes Hard Limits When to Switch

These are the five mistakes Veo 3 users make in the first two weeks. None of them are obvious from the documentation. They come from people burning through credits and posting what went wrong.

The Five Mistakes

Known failure modes.
Preventable every time.

These are documented from real community experience - not hypothetical edge cases. Every one has a specific fix.

01
Your clip comes back silent
This is the most-reported Veo 3 issue. The model generates audio from words - if you don't describe specific sounds, it often defaults to silence. Describing mood ("atmospheric," "cinematic") doesn't trigger audio generation. Neither does a visual-only prompt with no audio cues. Ingredients to Video and Scenebuilder modes are especially prone to dropping audio entirely.
✓ Fix: Name every sound you want by label - "rain on concrete," "espresso machine hiss," "soft lo-fi instrumental." Mood adjectives don't generate audio. Named sounds do. If you're using Scenebuilder, confirm audio is present in the preview before upscaling or extending - audio drops on upscale if it wasn't captured in the original generation.
02
Subtitles appearing on dialogue clips
Veo 3 was trained on a large volume of captioned video - YouTube, TikToks, gaming content. When it sees dialogue in a prompt, its default is often to burn in subtitles. Dialogue written in quotes ("She says 'hello'") is especially likely to trigger this, because quoted text looks like on-screen text to the model. Users have reported wasted credits from generations that are visually correct but subtitled over.
✓ Fix: Write dialogue after a colon, not in quotes: She says: Your line here. Add No subtitles. at the end of every prompt with speech. If subtitles persist, write it twice - once after the dialogue line, once at the very end. Repetition overrides the training bias.
03
Hitting the wrong Extend button
There are two Extend options in Google Flow and they use different models. The Extend button that appears directly under a generated clip defaults to Veo 2 Fast - no audio, lower quality. It's the obvious button and the wrong one. Users who extend from here get a clip that doesn't match the quality or audio of the original, burning Quality credits on a Fast output.
✓ Fix: For full Veo 3 quality and audio on extensions, always use the Extend option inside Scenebuilder - not the button under the clip. If you're already in Scenebuilder, use Frames to Video with the last frame saved as your start asset. Check the model shown on the generation before confirming.
04
Stacking camera movements
"Pan left while zooming in as the camera tracks the subject" produces a jittery mess that looks like a broken gimbal. Veo 3 can execute one camera movement well. It cannot execute three simultaneously. This is not a prompt wording problem - it is a model constraint. The same applies to combining slow motion with complex tracking.
✓ Fix: One camera move per prompt, written as its own sentence at the start of the prompt. "Slow dolly-in." "Static tripod." "Gentle pan left." If you need multiple moves, they go in separate clips chained in Scenebuilder.
05
Over-specifying audio conflicts with visuals
Writing a full sound design brief in the prompt ("dramatic orchestral swell building to a crescendo with low brass and percussion throughout the clip as the camera moves") causes the model to prioritize audio generation over visual coherence. The clip looks wrong. Practitioners consistently find that detailed audio directives degrade visual output quality.
✓ Fix: Keep audio cues sparse - one ambient description and one music or dialogue cue maximum. "Soft rain and distant traffic" works. A three-sentence sound design note does not. Handle detailed audio polishing in post-production.
Hard Limits | Not Prompt Problems

Things Veo 3
genuinely cannot do.

These aren't things that can be fixed with better prompting. They are architectural limits or policy decisions. Knowing them before you try saves credits and frustration.

✗
Clips longer than 8 seconds per generation
8 seconds is the hard cap per generation - on all plans, at all resolutions, on every access route. This is the firmest limit in the entire model. For longer content, chain clips in Google Flow's Scenebuilder using Frames to Video (Level 3 Playbook). Each Scenebuilder extension adds approximately 7 seconds, up to 148 seconds total from a single starting clip.
✗
Character memory between generations
Each Veo 3 generation is independent. The model has no memory of what a character looked like in a previous clip. Without Ingredients to Video (up to 3 reference images) or a verbatim character description block in every prompt, your character will look different in every generation. This is Veo 3's most cited production limitation and the reason the verbatim character rule (Playbook Level 3) is non-negotiable.
✗
Audio is not guaranteed - it's marked experimental
Google's own Flow changelog flags audio as a beta feature that "may not always return sound." This is not a prompt problem or a user error - the model can produce a visually correct clip with no audio even from a well-written prompt. Regenerating sometimes resolves it. If audio consistently drops on a specific mode (Ingredients, Scenebuilder), verify audio is present in the preview before committing to a Quality render.
✗
Image input in the EEA, Switzerland, or UK
Photo-to-video (Ingredients to Video, Frames to Video) is currently unavailable in the European Economic Area, Switzerland, and the UK due to privacy regulations. Text-to-video still works in these regions - you can still generate clips from text prompts. But Levels 2 and 3 of the Playbook require image input and are partially blocked for users in these locations.
✗
Reliable on-screen text
Generating readable text within a video frame - signs, labels, titles, product names - is unreliable. Veo 3 will attempt it but misspellings, garbled characters, and incorrect fonts are common. If your content requires legible on-screen text, add it in post-production rather than prompting for it. This applies to product names, signs, branded overlays, and any text that needs to be accurate.

10 Questions Verified April 2026

Real problems, real answers. These are the questions Veo 3 users actually run into - not the ones covered in the marketing.

The most common Veo 3 problem. Audio is generated from words in your prompt - if you describe mood instead of specific sounds, the model often produces silence. "Atmospheric" and "cinematic" don't generate audio. Named sounds do.

Replace mood adjectives with explicit sound labels: rain on concrete, espresso machine hiss, soft lo-fi instrumental. If audio is still missing, it may be a server-side issue - regenerating the same prompt sometimes resolves it. Audio generation is still marked as a beta feature by Google, meaning it can fail even with a well-written prompt.

Two modes are especially prone to dropping audio: Ingredients to Video and Scenebuilder. If you're using either, confirm audio is present in the preview before committing to a Quality render.

Veo 3 was trained on huge volumes of captioned video - YouTube, TikTok, gaming content. When it sees dialogue, it defaults to burning in subtitles. The fix is one punctuation change: write dialogue after a colon, not in quotes.

Triggers subtitles: She says "hello"
Prevents subtitles: She says: hello

Also add No subtitles. at the end of any prompt with speech. If they still appear, write it twice - once after the dialogue line and once at the very end. Repetition overrides the training bias. Even a single set of quotes elsewhere in the prompt can be enough to trigger captions.

The 8-second cap is firm - it applies on all plans, at all resolutions, on every access route. You cannot generate a longer clip in a single pass. For longer content you chain clips in Google Flow's Scenebuilder: each new shot starts from the last frame of the previous one, so the sequence looks continuous.

Starting from an 8-second clip, you can chain up to 20 Scenebuilder extensions of approximately 7 seconds each - up to 148 seconds total. Use the Extend option inside Scenebuilder specifically. The Extend button that appears directly under a generated clip uses Veo 2 Fast - no audio, lower quality.

OwnershipGoogle's terms grant you usage rights to content you generate. You can publish, monetize, and use videos in client work. Google does not claim ownership of your output. However, US copyright law currently does not protect purely AI-generated content - copyright protection on AI-generated video is not fully established. If you're building commercial work with significant IP value, consult a lawyer before assuming full copyright.
Commercial usePermitted on paid plans (Google AI Pro and Ultra) and through third-party platforms on their paid tiers. YouTube monetization is allowed on paid plans - apply YouTube's altered content label during upload to disclose AI generation, as required. Free-tier outputs may have restrictions - check the specific platform's terms before publishing client or monetized work.
What you cannot doGenerate realistic depictions of identifiable real people without consent. Generate content that replicates copyrighted footage or characters. Attempt to remove the SynthID watermark. Content that violates Google's Generative AI Prohibited Use Policy will be refused and repeat attempts can result in account restrictions.

SynthID is Google DeepMind's invisible watermarking technology. Every Veo 3 generation carries it - it's embedded in the file data, not visible as a logo or overlay in the video itself. It survives common editing operations like cropping, color grading, and compression.

Does it affect your videos visually? No. Viewers cannot see it. It does not degrade quality.

Does it affect YouTube or social media publishing? No. Platforms that implement SynthID detection can identify the content as AI-generated, but this does not block publishing or monetization. YouTube's current policy requires you to disclose AI-generated content using their altered content label - SynthID doesn't do that automatically, you still need to self-disclose.

Is there also a visible watermark? Sometimes. On lower subscription tiers (Free, Pro), a small "veo" text appears in the bottom-right corner. This is separate from SynthID. Ultra subscribers generating through Google Flow avoid the visible watermark, except in regions where local regulations require it. SynthID is always present regardless of tier or region.

OpenAI shut down the Sora app on April 26, 2026. Veo 3 is the strongest like-for-like replacement for short cinematic work - and unlike Sora, it generates native audio in the same pass. Your existing Sora prompts will mostly work, but Veo 3 interprets them differently and a few adjustments make a significant difference.

The key difference: Sora was trained to interpret abstract, expressive language and fill in the gaps creatively. Veo 3 is more literal - it follows specific cinematic directions precisely, but underperforms on vague prompts. "A lonely figure walks through a desolate cityscape" works well in Sora. In Veo 3, it tends to produce flat results. The fix is simple: add a camera move, lighting description, and shot type.

What to add to any Sora prompt you're porting to Veo 3A camera instruction as its own sentence: Slow dolly-in. or Static wide shot.
A lighting description: overcast diffused light or warm golden hour side light
Named audio cues if you want sound: distant traffic hum, soft wind - Sora never generated audio, so this is new territory
If dialogue is involved: write it after a colon not in quotes, and add No subtitles. at the end

What Veo 3 does better than Sora: Native audio. Multi-shot chaining via Google Flow Scenebuilder. Stronger prompt adherence when you use specific cinematic language. Where Sora had an edge: Abstract and surreal prompts. Sora was more expressive with unusual creative briefs - Veo 3 handles those less predictably.

There are two different failure types and they have different fixes.

Content policy refusalGoogle's filters blocked your prompt. The generation fails immediately with a specific message. This happens with realistic depictions of real people, copyrighted characters, violence, explicit content, or certain sensitive topics.

Fix: rephrase using neutral, professional language. Replace subjective terms like "beautiful" with descriptive ones like "woman in her 30s in a grey jacket." Replace violent framing with neutral action framing. Replace character names with appearance descriptions. Avoid any trademark or brand name in the prompt.
Technical failure ("Something went wrong")A server-side error unrelated to your prompt. Takes the full generation time before returning a generic error - this is what distinguishes it from a policy block. Veo 3 experiences these during peak demand.

Fix: wait 5-10 minutes and regenerate the same prompt without changing it. If it fails repeatedly, try a simpler version of the prompt first to confirm the issue is not content-related. Note on credits: Google's official Flow Help states that failed generations due to low-quality audio are automatically refunded. Policy blocks are not automatically refunded - those require manual deletion of the failed generation card.
